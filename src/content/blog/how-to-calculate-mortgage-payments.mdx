Buying a home is one of the biggest financial decisions you'll ever make. But before you start going to open houses, you need to answer one critical question: **How much house can I actually afford?**

To answer that, you need to understand how mortgage payments work and how to calculate them.

## The Anatomy of a Mortgage Payment (PITI)

When you make your monthly payment to the bank, you aren't just paying back the money you borrowed. Your payment typically consists of four parts, often referred to as **PITI**:

1. **Principal:** The actual money you borrowed to buy the house.
2. **Interest:** The cost of borrowing that money from the lender.
3. **Taxes:** Property taxes collected by your local government.
4. **Insurance:** Homeowners insurance (and potentially Private Mortgage Insurance if your down payment was less than 20%).

## The Standard Amortization Formula

Banks use a specific formula called the amortization formula to ensure your loan is paid off exactly at the end of your term (e.g., 30 years), with a portion of each payment going toward interest and a portion going toward the principal.

Here is the formula for calculating just the Principal and Interest (P&I):

`M = P [ r(1 + r)^n ] / [ (1 + r)^n - 1 ]`

- **M** = Total monthly payment
- **P** = The principal loan amount
- **r** = Your monthly interest rate (annual rate divided by 12)
- **n** = Number of payments over the loan's lifetime (e.g., 30 years = 360 payments)

### Tips for Lowering Your Payment

If the number above gives you sticker shock, don't worry. Here are a few ways to bring that monthly payment down:

- **Save a larger down payment:** The more you put down, the less you borrow, lowering your principal (P).
- **Improve your credit score:** A better credit score can secure you a lower interest rate (r), saving you tens of thousands of dollars over the life of the loan.
- **Extend the loan term:** A 30-year mortgage will have much lower monthly payments than a 15-year mortgage (though you will pay more total interest over time).
